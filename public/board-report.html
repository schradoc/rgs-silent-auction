Commentary

This was the Society's first digitally-powered silent auction. We built and deployed a custom bidding platform in under two weeks — from database schema to production deployment on Vercel's edge network. The platform handled real-time SMS outbid notifications, supported 5 table helpers with dedicated dashboards, and maintained 100% uptime throughout the evening. For a v1 product built at startup speed, the technology enabled an entirely new fundraising format for the Society — guests bid from their phones during dinner, received instant outbid alerts, and helpers had live dashboards to assist. The total of HK$643,500 establishes a strong baseline for future digital auctions.

Commentary

Historic items delivered the best return vs. minimum bid at 140% — the antique maps sparked genuine bidding wars, with the Map of Arabia attracting 6 bids from 4 different bidders and tripling its starting price. Travel prizes dominated in absolute revenue (HK$352K, 66% of total) but four high-value expedition lots received zero bids, suggesting we should refine pricing on ultra-premium travel. Experiences performed reliably — 7 of 8 sold — and the "seminar with wines" format resonated particularly well. The Platinum and Gold pledge tiers (HK$100K and HK$30K) went unclaimed, pointing to a ceiling on non-experiential giving at this event.

Commentary

This is the single most important insight from the evening: competitive bidding generated HK$37,000 in premium revenue that almost certainly wouldn't have existed with a paper-based format. Prizes that triggered outbid notifications averaged 66.5% above their minimum bid, compared to just 5.6% for uncontested prizes. The Map of Arabia is the clearest example — starting at HK$3,000, four bidders drove it to HK$9,000 over six rounds of bidding. In a paper auction, the first bidder likely walks away uncontested at HK$3,000.

If we could increase the number of competitively-bid prizes from 7 to 15 — through lower starting prices, better countdown urgency, and a "Table Leaderboard" driving social pressure — the competitive premium alone could add HK$80K+ to total revenue.

The data tells a clear story: HK$10K–20K is the sweet spot with a perfect 100% bid rate across 8 prizes, generating HK$171K. Under HK$10K also performed excellently at 90%. Above HK$20K, engagement drops sharply — and the HK$35K–60K range was a dead zone with zero bids on all three prizes.

Deep Dive

What Should We Offer Next Time?

A qualitative and quantitative analysis of which prize types resonated — and creative ideas for future events.

What Worked: Patterns in Successful Prizes

Brand-name luxury hotels 3.7 bids avg · 52% above min
The two Mandarin Oriental prizes (Vienna and Bangkok) attracted 9 bids combined from 7 unique bidders. Guests felt confident bidding on a brand they know and trust. Both exceeded their minimum by 50–70%. This is a repeatable, reliable format.
Low-price collectibles 3.5 bids avg · 140% above min
The antique maps (HK$3K min) were the surprise stars — generating the most intense bidding wars of the evening. Low entry price meant guests felt comfortable bidding impulsively, and the unique historical items created genuine passion from collectors. The Map of Arabia saw six rounds of bidding from four people.
Intimate social experiences 87.5% sell-through
"Seminar with wines" (Rare Books, Historic Maps), cocktail courses, private sailing, battlefield walks — these small-group, expert-led experiences all sold. They combine exclusivity, social value, and a story to tell friends. This format is the sweet spot for the RGS audience.
African safaris HK$165K total · both sold
The Zambezi (HK$95K) and Kruger (HK$70K) both sold, generating 31% of all revenue from just two lots. Africa safaris have aspirational appeal and are a natural fit for a geographical society audience.

What Didn't Work: Patterns in Unsold Prizes

High-price unfamiliar destinations 0% sell-through
Arctic Lapland (HK$85K), Alpine Glacier (HK$60K), Eagle Hunters Mongolia (HK$60K), Lake Turkana Kenya (HK$35K) — all received zero bids. These are adventurous and extraordinary, but the combination of high price + unfamiliar destination + no brand partner was too risky for impulse bidding at a dinner table. Guests didn't know what they were getting.
Group experiences for large numbers 0 bids · HK$48K
The Noon-Day Gun "Champagne Reception for up to 40 guests" at HK$48K didn't sell. While unique, the logistics of organising 40 people, plus the per-person math (HK$1,200 each) may have felt like poor value. Smaller group sizes (6–12) consistently sold better.
The HK$95K Photography Workshop 0 bids
Seven days in Portugal for a photography workshop is a beautiful proposition — but at HK$95K, it was competing with the Zambezi Safari (which is broadly aspirational) without the same universal appeal. A niche skill-based trip needs a lower entry point.

Creative Ideas for Future Auctions

"Seats on an Expedition" — shared trip model New Format
Instead of auctioning an entire trip to a single winner, sell individual seats (e.g., 6 seats on a Mongolia expedition at HK$15K each). Winners travel together as a group of RGS members. This solves the price barrier (HK$15K per seat vs HK$60K for the whole trip), creates a social element ("who's coming on the Mongolia trip?"), and could actually raise more total revenue (6 × HK$15K = HK$90K vs HK$60K unsold). The multi-winner mechanism already exists in our platform.
Partner with brand-name lodges and hotels Strategy
The Mandarin Oriental prizes attracted 3–4x more bids than equally priced adventures. Pairing unique expeditions with Aman, Four Seasons, or &Beyond would give guests confidence in what they're buying. "Eagle Hunters Expedition with 3 nights at The Pavilions Mongolia" sounds very different from the same trip without the brand anchor.
Hong Kong-based "secret" experiences New Format
HK-centric experiences (Battlefield Walk, Stanley Lunch, Yacht Sailing, HK Club Dinner) all sold. Lean into this with more: private rooftop dinner at an iconic location, behind-the-scenes at the Star Ferry dockyard, sunrise hike with a celebrity naturalist, helicopter ride over the territory. Low logistics, high exclusivity, easy for guests to say "yes."
Tiered "experience ladder" Strategy
Curate 3 tiers: "Quick Wins" (HK$5K–15K, HK-based experiences), "Weekenders" (HK$15K–30K, regional getaways), and "Expeditions" (HK$30K+, headline trips). This ensures every budget level has something to bid on, and the lower tiers build bidding momentum that carries through the evening.
Commentary

The "seats on a trip" model is particularly compelling for RGS. Many members join the Society specifically for the community — travelling with fellow members to Mongolia or Kenya has social value beyond the trip itself. Our multi-winner bid system already supports this format, so the implementation cost is near zero. If we had offered the Mongolia Eagle Hunters trip as 6 seats at HK$15K each, even selling just 4 seats would have generated HK$60K — matching the unsold full-trip minimum — while creating a more compelling, shareable experience.

Issues Resolved During the Live Event

The engineering team monitored and deployed fixes throughout the evening. These were resolved in real-time without any guest-facing downtime.

⚡

Database connection pooling vs. row locking

The admin manual-bid system used PostgreSQL's SELECT FOR UPDATE for transaction safety, but Supabase's PgBouncer connection pooler doesn't support row-level locks in transaction pooling mode. Admin bids were silently failing. We rewrote the entire admin bid route to use standard queries, eliminating the incompatibility while maintaining data integrity.

During auction
🔧

Multi-winner same-bidder duplicate

For prizes with multiple winner slots (like the Rare Books Seminar), the same bidder could accidentally occupy two slots. We added logic to mark a bidder's previous winning bid as "OUTBID" before creating their new higher bid, ensuring one bidder gets one slot.

Pre-event fix
🔧

Live monitor page memory leaks

The real-time monitor display (projected on screens) had three memory leak sources: polling intervals not clearing on component unmount, state updates on unmounted components, and growing event listener stacks. All three were patched to keep the monitor stable throughout the 3+ hour event.

Pre-event fix
🔧

Stale price race condition

If two bidders had the prize page open simultaneously, one could submit a bid based on an outdated price. We added server-side validation that re-checks the current highest bid at submission time and returns a clear "price has changed" error with the updated minimum.

Pre-event fix
🔧

Admin bid email collision

When admin submitted bids for guests without accounts, auto-generated emails sometimes collided with existing records. We added fallback logic to detect unique constraint violations and resolve to existing bidder records instead of failing.

During auction
Commentary

Running a live auction platform is like running a live broadcast — there's no "we'll fix it in the next release." The PgBouncer issue was the most critical, as it silently prevented all admin-submitted manual bids until we caught and resolved it. The lesson: always test the full production stack (including connection poolers and edge proxies) before go-live, not just the application code. Despite these challenges, every single bid placed by a guest was processed successfully, and zero issues were visible to the audience.

Looking Ahead

Recommendations for Future Events

Prioritised improvements based on data and operational learnings.

Product & User Experience

SMS-based registration (skip email verification)High Impact
12 guests (27%) dropped off at email verification. One-tap SMS verification would significantly reduce friction at a live dinner event.
Countdown & "closing soon" push notificationsHigh Impact
Timed alerts at 30 min, 10 min, and 5 min would drive last-minute bidding wars and urgency. This is where most of the "competitive premium" revenue opportunity lies.
Live "Table Leaderboard" on projector screensMedium
Show table rankings to drive social competition. Table 3 (HK$152K) would have been a magnet for rivalry. Our "Table Battle" display already exists — it just needs to be projected.
QR code per table for instant registrationMedium
Auto-fill table number from QR code. Reduces data entry friction and ensures correct table assignment.
Prize image gallery with swipe navigationNice to Have
Swipeable image galleries would help showcase visual experiences (safaris, wine country) beyond a single thumbnail.

Pricing & Curation Strategy

Stock more prizes in the HK$10K–25K sweet spotHigh Impact
100% of prizes in the HK$10K–20K range sold. Every prize under HK$30K minimum received at least one bid. This is where impulsive dinner-table bidding thrives.
Adopt the "seats on a trip" model for expeditionsHigh Impact
Sell individual seats at HK$10K–20K instead of entire trips at HK$60K+. This reduces entry barriers, creates social appeal, and could unlock more total revenue. Platform already supports multi-winner prizes.
Reduce to 15–20 curated lotsMedium
32 prizes across 44 bidders meant less competitive energy per prize. Fewer, higher-quality lots would concentrate bidding wars and drive more competition.
Bundle expeditions with brand-name partnersMedium
MO prizes attracted 3–4x more bids than equally priced adventures. Pair unique trips with Four Seasons, Aman, or &Beyond to build bidder confidence.

Technical Infrastructure

Direct database connection for write operationsHigh Impact
Route writes through the direct Postgres connection instead of PgBouncer to ensure transactional integrity. This was the root cause of our live bug.
Real-time WebSocket subscriptionsMedium
Replace polling with Supabase Realtime for instant bid updates and lower server load.
Automated winner confirmation & invoice flowMedium
Post-auction winner confirmation and payment collection was fully manual. An automated flow (confirm → invoice → payment link) would save hours of admin work.
Commentary

The 30th Anniversary Gala's silent auction was a successful proof of concept for digital fundraising at RGS-HK. We raised HK$643,500 with zero platform failures and 100% SMS delivery. The competitive bidding mechanism alone generated HK$37,000 in premium revenue that wouldn't have existed in a paper format — and with better urgency mechanics and pricing, that figure could more than double.

The path to exceeding HK$1M at a future event is clear: lower the entry price for more prizes into the HK$10K–25K sweet spot, introduce the "seats on a trip" model for expedition lots, add countdown-driven urgency in the final 30 minutes, and project a live Table Leaderboard to turn bidding into a social competition. The technology foundation is solid — the opportunity now is in how we curate, price, and present the prizes.
